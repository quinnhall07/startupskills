# JTBD Protocols

Attribution: Tony Ulwick (*Jobs to be Done: Theory to Practice*, Strategyn), Clayton Christensen (*Competing Against Luck*), Bob Moesta (*Demand-Side Sales*), Alan Klement (*When Coffee and Kale Compete*). Two schools, one interview protocol. This reference distills the operating method; the source texts are short and worth re-reading every quarter.

## Purpose

Two schools of Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD), unified by the **Switch Interview**. The Mom Test is the right tool *before* a purchase exists — discovering problems in the customer's world. JTBD is the right tool *after* a purchase exists — reconstructing the real timeline of why someone switched. Use both. They are complementary, not competing.

## The two schools — be explicit about which you are using

**(a) Ulwick — Outcome-Driven Innovation (jobs-as-activities).** A job is a functional task the customer is trying to execute ("get my lawn cut," "transfer money to a contractor," "onboard a new sales rep"). The job is decomposed into measurable **desired outcomes** ("minimize the time to mow," "minimize the amount of edge trimming," "minimize the variability in cut quality"). Quantitative. Survey-driven *after* qualitative interviews establish the outcomes list. Best for: established markets, feature prioritization, B2B SaaS roadmapping. Source: https://strategyn.com/jobs-to-be-done/ and the free PDF at https://jobs-to-be-done-book.com/.

**(b) Christensen / Moesta / Klement — Jobs-as-Progress.** A job is the *progress* a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance ("feel like a good parent during my commute," "feel competent at my new job"). Emotional and social, not just functional. Best for: new markets, switching behavior, consumer products, narrative positioning. Source: Klement's two-interpretations essay https://jtbd.info/know-the-two-very-different-interpretations-of-jobs-to-be-done-5a18b748bd89.

The two schools answer different questions. Ulwick answers "which feature next?" Christensen/Moesta answer "why did they switch, and how do we tell that story back?" Choose the school that matches the question.

## The Switch Interview (Bob Moesta, ~47 minutes, 10 phases)

The canonical JTBD interview. The goal is to reconstruct a real purchase decision in forensic detail — the *timeline* from first thought to current use. Two interviewers preferred (one asks, one notes timeline). Recorded. Past tense, specific dates and places.

1. **Set the stage** (2m). Rapport. Explain you want to walk through the story of how they came to use the product. No selling, no pitching.
2. **Pre-purchase world** (2m). "What was your life like before?" Establish the baseline state.
3. **First thought** (3m). "When did the idea of finding something different first enter your head?" Pin the first-thought moment to a specific event.
4. **The struggle** (4m). "What was frustrating about the old way? What broke?" Surface the **push** of the situation.
5. **Solution search** (5m). "What did you Google? Who did you ask? What tabs did you have open?" Reconstruct the search path.
6. **Product selection** (5m). "Why this one, not the alternatives? What almost stopped you?" Surface **anxiety** and the deciding moment.
7. **Early experience** (5m). "What was the first week like? What surprised you?" The first-use moment is when the **pull** is confirmed or shattered.
8. **Usage evolution** (7m). "How has it changed over months? When did it become a habit?" Map the path to retention.
9. **Current friction** (9m). "What frustrates you now? What would make you leave?" Surfaces the next switch.
10. **Four-Forces synthesis** (5m). Score push / pull / anxiety / habit on 0–10. Confirm with the interviewee.

Klement's script: https://jtbd.info/a-script-to-kickstart-your-jobs-to-be-done-interviews-2768164761d7. Moesta on Intercom: https://www.intercom.com/blog/podcasts/bob-moesta-on-unpacking-customer-motivations-with-jobs-to-be-done/.

## The Four Forces of Progress (Moesta)

Every switch is governed by four forces. Two push toward the new. Two anchor to the old.

- **Push of the current situation.** Pain with the status quo. The thing that broke yesterday.
- **Pull of the new solution.** Vision of what's better. The promised future state.
- **Anxiety about the new.** Fear of switching — "what if it doesn't work?" "what if I look stupid?"
- **Habit of the present.** Inertia. The cost of changing routines. *Usually the strongest force.*

**The equation: switch happens only when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.**

This is not a metaphor; it is how you decide what to fix. Diagram from JTBD Radio: https://jobstobedone.org/radio/unpacking-the-progress-making-forces-diagram/.

## Sample Switch Interview questions — verbatim-usable

- "Walk me back to the day you decided to look for something different. What happened that day?"
- "What were you using before? What was frustrating about it?"
- "What did you type into Google? Who did you ask?"
- "What almost made you NOT buy this?"
- "When did you first realize this was the right choice?"
- "What was the first month of use like? What surprised you?"
- "If you had to leave this product tomorrow, what would the trigger be?"

## When JTBD beats Mom Test

- Customer has already made a purchase decision — there is a real timeline to reconstruct.
- You want to understand **why people switched** (post-purchase) and use the answer for positioning.
- You need data for cold-email subject lines and landing-page headlines — the four forces *are* the messaging.
- You have a working product and need to sharpen the narrative, not discover the problem.

## When Mom Test beats JTBD

- Pre-purchase. No transaction yet to reconstruct.
- Early-stage idea validation. No customers exist.
- You don't yet know which problem to solve — Mom Test (and Indi Young thinking-style interviews) surface the problem space; JTBD only works *after* a purchase.

## Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) — when and how

Use ODI when you already understand the job and now need to prioritize features.

- Interview 8–12 customers qualitatively to enumerate the **desired outcomes** for the job. An outcome is phrased as a direction-of-improvement statement: "minimize the time to…", "minimize the likelihood that…", "increase the stability of…".
- Survey 100+ customers on each outcome: importance (0–10) and current satisfaction (0–10).
- **Opportunity score formula**: `Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)`. Score 10+ = high opportunity (customers care, current solutions fail). Score 12+ = extreme opportunity.
- Output: ranked list of underserved outcomes → product roadmap.

Best for: B2B SaaS feature prioritization, established markets, mature products. Reference: https://strategyn.com/jobs-to-be-done/ and the free book.

## Alan Klement — Jobs-as-Progress (*When Coffee and Kale Compete*)

The job is **progress**, not a task. People hire products to make progress in their lives — to move from a current state to a desired state. The competition is **anything that gets the customer the same progress**, including doing nothing.

- Tesla competes with not-just-cars: status, lifestyle, environmental-identity products. The job is "feel like the person who chose the future."
- Mailchimp competes with not-just-email-tools: the bookkeeper who used to do the newsletter, the spreadsheet that tracked the list, doing nothing at all.

Sharpens `idea-genesis`: don't ask "what's the problem"; ask "what progress is the customer trying to make in their life, and what are they hiring something to achieve?"

## Output format — Forces of Progress Diagram

For each customer cohort, score the four forces 0–10 from the interview transcripts:

```
Push   (pain with status quo):     7
Pull   (vision of new):            9
Anxiety (fear of switching):       4
Habit  (inertia of present):       6

Push + Pull     = 16
Anxiety + Habit = 10
→ 16 > 10 → switch likely.
```

If the equation flips, identify *which* force is preventing the switch and address it explicitly:
- High anxiety → testimonials, money-back guarantee, free trial, white-glove onboarding.
- High habit → trigger event marketing (catch them at the moment routine breaks), migration tools.
- Low push → wait. Or change segment. You cannot manufacture pain.
- Low pull → sharpen the promise. The headline is wrong.

## `pmf-audit` application

Interview 10 recent *switchers* AND 10 recent *churners*. Score the forces for each cohort.

- If switchers score Push+Pull >> Anxiety+Habit (16+ vs <8): strong PMF, the story is clear, scale the message.
- If switchers score Push+Pull barely > Anxiety+Habit (within 4): fragile PMF, every churn risk is real, fix the weakest force before scaling.
- If churners score Anxiety+Habit > Push+Pull at any point post-purchase: the product failed to confirm the pull. The first-week experience is broken.

A fragile PMF that scales becomes an expensive PMF that churns. Diagnose with the forces before pouring fuel.
