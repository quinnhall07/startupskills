# Case Studies — Runtime Pattern-Match Library

Ten companies as anchor cases for skills to reference at runtime. Each is 100–150 words, distilled to the single load-bearing lesson. Skills also web-search for fresh analogues at runtime; this library is the always-loaded baseline.

## Stripe — Founder-market fit and things that don't scale

Patrick and John Collison built Stripe because they had personally hit the pain of accepting payments online from prior startups. The first version had no automated bank setup — Patrick and John manually faxed forms to banks each night. The product launched only for YC startups; the Collisons would walk new founders through integration, sometimes installing it themselves on the customer's laptop (this is now called "Collison Installation"). They charged from day one, were *more* expensive than competitors at launch, and never apologized for the price. The lessons: ridiculously narrow first cohort; manual delivery of what would later be automated; founder-credibility-as-distribution; charge from the start. Source: PG essays, Patrick Collison interviews, the YC corpus.

## Brex — Pivot from VR to corporate cards via founder-market fit

Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi went into YC building a VR headset. Inside the batch they realized they hated VR but loved fintech — and they had built EBANX, a Brazilian payments processor, before college. They pivoted to corporate credit cards for startups. The pivot worked because the team had earned FMF in fintech years earlier; the VR play had none. Brex became a unicorn by closing two ICP-adjacent gaps (no personal guarantee, instant approval for startups with capital in the bank). Lesson: a pivot to where you have FMF beats persistence in a space where you don't. Source: Dalton's *All About Pivoting*.

## Airbnb — Narrow start, multiple launches, things that don't scale

The first AirBedAndBreakfast.com sold air mattresses to design-conference attendees in San Francisco when hotels sold out. The founders launched three times before traction. To improve listings, Brian Chesky flew to New York and personally photographed hosts' apartments — manual, doesn't scale, decisive. The narrow start (conferences) gave them a forcing function the open marketplace wouldn't have. Lessons: launch is not an event, it's a sequence; narrow until you have signal; do the thing that doesn't scale until it does. Source: PG *Do Things That Don't Scale*, Chesky interviews.

## Segment — Pivot to data infrastructure

Segment started as ClassMetric, a classroom feedback tool that nobody used. The team pivoted multiple times before noticing that the analytics code they kept writing themselves was the actual product. The pivot wasn't sentimental — they let the original idea die and built what they'd repeatedly needed. Acquired by Twilio for $3.2B. Lesson: the side-task you keep building for yourself can be the company. Watch what you reach for. Source: founder writeups, First Round Review.

## Slack — Pivot from Glitch

Stewart Butterfield's team built Glitch, a multiplayer game. It didn't work as a business. Butterfield noticed they had built an excellent internal communication tool for their own team — and that other companies might pay for it. The Glitch team's IRC bot replacement became Slack. Lesson: pivots are not failures; many of the most valuable companies started elsewhere. Don't burn the team or the cap table; redirect them. Source: Butterfield writeup, *Hard Thing About Hard Things* adjacent.

## Superhuman — PMF engineering via the Sean Ellis survey

Rahul Vohra ran the Sean Ellis "How would you feel if you could no longer use Superhuman?" survey when he had thousands of users. Result: 22% "very disappointed." Below the 40% threshold. Rather than launch broadly, Vohra segmented the "very disappointed" cohort (the High-Expectation Customer — founders and executives who lived in email), doubled down on what they loved (speed), and built features to convert "somewhat disappointed" fence-sitters. Quarterly re-runs lifted the score from 22% to 58%. Lessons: PMF is engineered, not stumbled into; segment to the HXC; ignore the "not disappointed" cohort entirely. Source: Vohra's First Round Review article.

## PlanGrid — Domain expertise + schlep-heavy B2B

Tracy Young worked in construction. She knew that blueprints on construction sites lived as paper rolls, got marked up, and were a logistical nightmare. She built PlanGrid (with a technical cofounder) to put blueprints on iPads. Construction is the canonical schlep — regulated, fragmented, full of users who don't want new software. PlanGrid's wedge was Tracy's specific scars and her ability to sit on a construction site demoing to foremen. Acquired by Autodesk for ~$875M. Lessons: domain depth is the moat; the schlep is the moat; non-technical-founder + technical-cofounder works when the non-technical founder has lived the pain. Source: YC alumni interviews.

## DoorDash — Landing page + manual delivery

Tony Xu and team built PaloAltoDelivery.com with a static page listing local restaurants and a phone number. When someone ordered, the founders drove to the restaurant, picked up the food, and delivered it themselves. No driver network, no dispatching software — just the founders doing the work. The system tested whether the demand was real (it was) and gave them ground-truth knowledge of every step in the delivery workflow. Lessons: Wizard of Oz before software; founder-delivers-the-service is the fastest path to operational truth; charge from the first order. Source: YC interviews, *Do Things That Don't Scale*.

## Twitch — Long iteration, content + gaming wedge

Justin.tv launched in 2007 as a 24/7 broadcast of Justin Kan's life. It generalized to live streaming for anyone. Most categories died. Gaming, where streamers and viewers had latent communities and high engagement, grew. The team eventually shut down Justin.tv and renamed the company Twitch, focusing entirely on the gaming wedge. Acquired by Amazon for $970M. Lesson: long iteration with directional honesty beats locking into a thesis. The wedge that worked wasn't the original; the team's willingness to cut the rest was the decisive move. Source: founder interviews, YC corpus.

## Magic — Viral consumer prototype, attitudinal-vs-retention trap

Magic launched in 2015 as an SMS-based concierge — text anything and a human team would arrange it. The launch went viral; users texted within hours of seeing the Hacker News post. Retention was the problem: the unit economics didn't work and most users churned after the novelty. The team pivoted multiple times (eventually to a remote-assistant platform). Lesson: viral demand is not retention; attitudinal "this is cool" attention does not equal behavioral commitment to use. Watch what users do in week 4, not week 1. Source: Magic founder writeups, YC corpus.

## How skills use this file

When a skill encounters a founder situation, it pattern-matches: "Your background is structurally similar to Tracy Young at PlanGrid — domain expert with technical cofounder…", or "This is structurally similar to the Magic launch — viral attitudinal but no retention data yet, here's what to watch in week 4." Real anchors beat abstract advice. When the founder's situation doesn't match any of these ten, the skill should web-search for a fresh analogue rather than force a match.
