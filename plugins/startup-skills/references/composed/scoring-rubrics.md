# Scoring Rubrics

Two rubrics. Dalton Caldwell's four-criteria scoring (for fast quantitative pressure-testing) and the YC ten-question framework (for the qualitative narrative around the score). Skills use both: the 4-criteria for the composite recommendation, the 10-question framework for the per-dimension paragraphs.

## Dalton's 4-Criteria Rubric

Attribution: Dalton Caldwell, YC partner, *How to Get and Evaluate Startup Ideas* and *Where Do Great Startup Ideas Come From*. Score each dimension 1–10.

### 1. Market size

- **10** — multi-billion-dollar venture-backable space with clear current demand (e.g., payments infrastructure when Stripe started).
- **7** — large addressable market, growing, with clear pockets of acute need.
- **5** — meaningful niche, but growth depends on a thesis about future shape.
- **3** — small or shrinking market, or one that's structurally hard to capture (fragmented, low-willingness-to-pay).
- **1** — too small to support a venture outcome even if you win it entirely.

Note: a small market that is growing fast can score higher than a large market that is flat. Time-derivatives matter.

### 2. Founder-market fit (FMF)

**The single most important dimension.** Why are *you* the right person to solve this?

- **10** — domain expert with deep operational scars, unique network, and the technical or product capacity to ship. The kind of fit Tracy Young had with construction at PlanGrid.
- **7** — strong domain or technical edge, plus a clear plan to acquire the missing leg in months not years.
- **5** — moderate fit — interested but not lived, or technical without domain access.
- **3** — neither domain nor distribution edge. Pure curiosity.
- **1** — actively poor fit (selling to a customer type you don't understand, in a market with strong gatekeepers).

### 3. Ease of getting started

How fast can you produce behavioral evidence (not perfect product, just *behavior*)?

- **10** — landing page + cold outreach today; first paid customer plausible in two weeks.
- **7** — Fake Door or Concierge MVP feasible in a week; first paid customer in 4–6 weeks.
- **5** — requires modest build (~4 weeks) before any signal possible.
- **3** — months of build before any test — regulated industries, hardware, two-sided marketplaces.
- **1** — capital-intensive or licensing-gated; no behavioral signal possible without massive upfront cost.

### 4. Early market feedback

What does the evidence so far actually say?

- **10** — multiple customers committed money or substantial time before product existed.
- **7** — multiple 0.6+ behavioral signals; clear workflow displacement requests.
- **5** — mixed signal; some behavioral, some attitudinal.
- **3** — only attitudinal so far ("sounds great"), no behavioral evidence.
- **1** — explicit rejection from target ICP, or only friend-and-family enthusiasm.

## Composite recommendation rules

After scoring all four dimensions:

- **FMF ≥ 7 AND ≥ 2 other dimensions ≥ 7** → "Worth pursuing. Name the single largest gap and a specific test to close it."
- **Tar pit detected** (per `references/tar-pit-detection.md`) → "Tar pit. Here is why specifically. Move on."
- **FMF < 5** → "No founder-market fit. Find a cofounder who has it, or change ideas. The system won't help you continue here without an FMF plan."
- **Otherwise** → "Mixed. Here are 2–3 things we'd need to learn next to make this clear. Do this before any building."

The composite is not an average. FMF is veto-weighted: a 9 in Market with a 3 in FMF is not "worth pursuing" — it's a setup for the wrong founder shipping the wrong product in a real market.

## YC 10-Question Framework

Attribution: Dalton Caldwell, *How to Get and Evaluate Startup Ideas*. Use to write one paragraph of qualitative analysis per question alongside the numeric scores.

1. **Founder-market fit.** Most important. Why you?
2. **Market size — now.** Is it big now, or small but fast-growing?
3. **Market growth — future.** Trajectory matters more than current size for early-stage.
4. **Acuteness of the problem.** Is this hair-on-fire, or "would be nice to have"?
5. **Existence of competition.** Existing competitors is usually a *good* sign. No competition usually means no market.
6. **Are you the right team?** Distinct from FMF — covers technical capacity, distribution, capital plan.
7. **Did you genuinely have this problem?** "Make something I want" is more reliable than "make something they might want."
8. **Was it your idea, or did someone else have it first?** The point isn't novelty; it's whether you've thought about it long enough.
9. **When did you have the idea?** Sustained obsession is a tell. Yesterday's idea is suspect.
10. **Is this a good idea space?** Even if v1 is wrong, are adjacent ideas fertile? Idea spaces matter more than specific ideas.

## Using both rubrics together

- Score the 4 criteria first to get a composite recommendation.
- Then walk the 10 questions to write the narrative. The narrative explains the scores and surfaces the gap to close.
- If the narrative contradicts the scores (e.g., you scored Market Size at 7 but cannot answer Question 2 specifically), trust the narrative and revise the score down. Confidence requires evidence.
