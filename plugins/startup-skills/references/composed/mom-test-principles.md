# Mom Test Principles

Attribution: Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test* (2013). The shortest, most operationally rigorous text in the founder canon. Read once before starting customer discovery; re-read every two months. This reference distills the operating principles; it is not a substitute for the book.

## The premise

If you ask your mom whether she'd buy your business idea, she'll say yes — to be polite. The Mom Test is a set of question-design rules that prevent the founder from collecting compliments, and that work even if you accidentally ask your literal mom. The goal of every customer conversation is to learn what the customer *does* and *has done*, not what they *think* or would *want*.

## The three rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The moment you pitch, the conversation contaminates. Hold your idea back. Ask about the customer's world, their tools, their workarounds, their last hard week. Pitching is for sales, not discovery.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future.** "Would you use this?" is hypothetical and worthless. "Tell me about the last time you needed to do X" is data. Past behavior predicts future behavior; future intent predicts almost nothing.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** If you're talking, you're not learning. The founder default is to fill silence, explain, qualify, lead the witness. Resist. Let the customer's pauses be the customer's pauses.

## Bad question types — never ask

- **Hypothetical.** "Would you use this if it existed?" — Politeness response. Weight 0.0.
- **Leading.** "Don't you think it's a problem that…?" — You wrote their answer for them. Weight 0.0.
- **Yes/no questions about value.** "Do you have this problem?" — Answers are noisy because customers don't want to seem out of touch.
- **Feature-focused.** "What features would you want?" — Asks the customer to do the founder's job. The wishlist is noise.
- **Price hypotheticals.** "How much would you pay?" — Anchors to compliance. Wait until there's a real offer.
- **Pitching disguised as a question.** "We're building X — does that sound useful?" — The pitch corrupts the data.

## Good question types — always available

- **Past-behavior.** "Walk me through the last time you did X." "What did you do yesterday around this?"
- **Time-and-money already spent.** "How long does this take you each week?" "What have you spent trying to solve it so far?"
- **Current workaround.** "How are you solving this today?" "What tools do you use? Show me — share your screen."
- **Other people involved.** "Who else is part of solving this? Who do they roll up to? Who actually pays the bill?"
- **Severity probes.** "What happens if it just keeps being broken? What did you do the last time it broke?"
- **History.** "How did you find your current solution? How long have you used it? Why did the prior one not work?"
- **Concrete commitments — the closing question.** "Would you introduce me to two other people who have this problem?" / "Would you give me $X to do this manually for a week?" / "Can I sit in on your workflow next Tuesday?" — A `yes` is signal. A polite `let me check` and silence after is the truth.

## The three bad data types to discard

1. **Compliments.** "Cool idea." "I love that you're working on this." Weight 0.0. Note them only to remember that the conversation happened; do not treat them as evidence.
2. **Hypothetical fluff.** "I might use that." "I'd definitely consider it." Weight 0.0.
3. **Wishlists / feature requests.** "You should add X." Useful only as a clue that the customer's deeper problem is adjacent — drill into *why* they want X, never as a roadmap input.

## The Mom Test endgame: commitment and currency

A good customer conversation ends with the customer paying you something — money, time, or social capital. Money is highest signal. Time is next (an hour of their schedule, access to their workflow, a recording of their screen). Social capital is third (introductions to other prospects, an internal mention to their boss). If the customer leaves with nothing committed, the meeting is "polite" — the signal is at most weight 0.3.

Founders defer the close because closing feels uncomfortable. Closing IS the discovery question. Without it, you have a hangout, not data.

## Application across Startup Skills

- `problem-focus` uses these rules when sharpening hypothesis questions for the founder to take into interviews.
- `discovery-coach` PREPARE mode generates interview scripts entirely from these rules — and explicitly refuses to include bad-question types.
- `discovery-coach` DEBRIEF mode applies these rules in reverse: every statement the customer made is classified by which rule it answers (past behavior = signal; future intent = noise).
- `signal-audit` uses the Mom Test framing when computing whether evidence in the log is real or polite.

Re-read Fitzpatrick before every batch of 5+ interviews. The book is short. The discipline drifts fast.
