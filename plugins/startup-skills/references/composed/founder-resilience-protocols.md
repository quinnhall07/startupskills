# Founder Resilience Protocols

This reference is loaded by the `founder-resilience` skill and by any decision-heavy skill that runs after weeks of pressure (`pivot-decision`, `cofounder-fit`, `pmf-audit`). Attribution: Ben Horowitz (*The Hard Thing About Hard Things*), Paul Graham ("Determination"), Annie Duke (*Thinking in Bets*, *Quit*), and the founder mental-health literature curated by Founders Network and First Round Review.

## Why this is decision quality, not self-care

Founder resilience is not a wellness topic. It is a decision-hygiene topic. The same data produces different decisions depending on whether the founder is rested or depleted, and most consequential startup decisions are made under fatigue. Founder anecdotal consensus puts the figure north of 65% — the survey-cited stat circulates in YC alumni channels but has not been independently published; treat as directional, not precise. Three structural consequences follow:

1. **Decision quality under fatigue collapses non-linearly.** A tired founder doesn't make slightly worse decisions; they make categorically worse ones, because the System 2 audit pathway is the first thing fatigue degrades (Kahneman, *Thinking Fast and Slow*).
2. **Loneliness suppresses dissent-seeking behavior.** Depleted founders stop calling the advisors who push back, and start calling the ones who validate. The bias sentinel cannot help if the founder isn't surfacing dissent in the first place.
3. **The aggressive epistemic auditor pattern fails on a depleted founder.** Skills that depend on the founder engaging with friction (`pivot-decision`, `pmf-audit`, `discovery-coach`) degrade silently when the human on the other side is too exhausted to argue back productively.

The implication: before any consequential skill runs, the founder's state is part of the input. If they are visibly depleted, the right next move is often *not* to answer the question they asked.

## Sustainable-pace check (run every 4 weeks)

One question: **Can you maintain your current burn rate — mental and financial — for 18 months?**

Startup timelines are always longer than the founder's plan. The PG / Sam Altman corpus is consistent: assume 2-3x your stated timeline to PMF. If the honest answer is no, that is a decision-making constraint, not an inspirational obstacle to push through. The reframe is structural: **pace IS strategy.** A founder who flames out at month 9 of an 18-month problem has lost, regardless of how hard they pushed in months 1-8.

## The decision-fatigue protocol (per Annie Duke, *Thinking in Bets*)

If the founder is making a go/no-go decision — kill, pivot, continue, fire, hire, raise — AND is visibly exhausted: **sleep first.**

- If the decision is the same tomorrow, it's right.
- If it changes after sleep, you were just tired.
- **The 24-hour rule:** irreversible decisions wait 24 hours after the trigger emotion. Reversible decisions can move faster, but irreversible ones (firing a cofounder, killing a product line, accepting a term sheet) require the cooling interval. Source: Duke, plus the canonical "don't send the email" advice across the operator corpus.

Skills should refuse to ratify an irreversible decision made within 24 hours of an emotional trigger. The skill's job in that moment is to schedule the decision, not to execute it.

## Cofounder alignment check (one-line diagnostic)

Every cofounder, asked separately, should be able to complete this sentence the same way:

> **"My cofounder and I both believe our biggest risk is _______ and our biggest advantage is _______."**

If the two answers diverge, the cofounders are operating on different theories of the business — and the divergence will surface at the worst possible moment (term sheet, pivot, layoff). Fix it now, when the stakes are low. See `cofounder-frameworks.md` for the 4-week trial protocol; this one-line check is the running diagnostic *after* the trial, used quarterly.

## You are not your company (Horowitz, Andreessen)

The four reframes that load-bearing operators repeat to themselves:

1. The company is a separate legal and operational entity from you. Its failure is not a referendum on your worth as a person.
2. You can — and at some point must — hire people smarter than you at parts of the job. Refusing to is ego, not standards.
3. The company will eventually not need you for everything. That is success, not displacement.
4. Horowitz's framing: *"The struggle is not failure. The struggle is what makes greatness possible."* The struggle is the job, not a sign you're doing the job wrong.

## The loneliness diagnostic

By month 6-12 of a serious build, the founder is typically the only person who fully knows what's at stake. Team members see fragments. Family and friends are not peers in this role. Ben Horowitz's framing — paraphrased across multiple a16z appearances — is that loneliness is the founder's most under-diagnosed problem, because it doesn't present as loneliness; it presents as bad decisions and brittle behavior.

Counter-venues for actual peer contact:

- **YC Bookface** (alumni only).
- **Pavilion** (operators, post-PMF lean).
- **On Deck** (cohort-based, pre-PMF lean).
- **MicroConf** (bootstrapped SaaS).
- **Niche founder Discords** in the relevant vertical.
- **A single advisor or peer who has been to PMF, stalled, and recovered** — this profile is the highest-leverage relationship a founder can have, and is often free.

## The "relentlessly resourceful" reread (Paul Graham)

PG's *Determination* essay names the quality YC weights most heavily. The often-misread point: determination is *not* stubbornness. It is the willingness to push hard AND the willingness to update on evidence. Stubborn ≠ determined. Determination changes tactics when the evidence says to; stubbornness keeps pushing the same tactic harder. Knowing when *not* to resource something is part of being resourceful. Reread this essay every six months — the meaning changes as the founder's situation does.

## Burnout warning signs

These are the field-observed leading indicators, drawn from the Founders Network mental-health corpus and the First Round operator interviews. Any two appearing simultaneously should route the founder to a one-week break before any major decision is ratified:

- You're working long hours but not actually deciding anything.
- You stopped reading critical email (investor updates, top-customer threads).
- You stopped doing customer interviews because "I already know what they'll say."
- You feel relieved — not annoyed — when meetings get canceled.
- You're rationalizing why you don't need to update your beliefs on a key thesis.
- You're sleeping but not rested; or not sleeping at all.

When the skill detects these patterns in the conversation, it should name them and pause the decision queue rather than answer the question on the table.

## The "would you start this today?" reframe (Annie Duke, *Quit*)

The cleanest sunk-cost antidote in the operator literature:

> **If you were starting today, knowing what you know now, would you start this?**

- If yes — continue, and the work is real.
- If no — that is a kill signal, not a "push harder" signal.

Sunk cost is the loudest voice when the founder is fatigued; this reframe silences it by making the past irrelevant to the question on the table. Pair with the pre-mortem (Klein) for symmetric coverage — pre-mortem surfaces what could kill it, this question surfaces whether you'd start it.
