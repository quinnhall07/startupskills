# Tar Pit Detection

A **tar pit** is an idea that looks obvious and tempting but consistently fails because the underlying market structure is hostile. The acquisition costs are too high, retention churns out the cohort, monetization is structurally weak, or network effects work against the new entrant. Tar pits are *the* most expensive class of mistake in early-stage startups: founders sink 6–18 months because the idea seems like nobody has tried it. Almost always, dozens have tried it. They lost.

Attribution: Dalton Caldwell, YC partner, has talked about tar pit ideas across multiple Startup School videos. The system here surfaces the canonical list plus the structural reasoning so a founder can pattern-match their idea before committing.

## Decision rule

If a candidate idea matches a known tar pit AND the founder cannot articulate a *specific* insider edge — a regulatory advantage, a private dataset, a captive distribution channel, a deep operational scar nobody else has — kill it. Move on.

If a founder claims "I can't believe no one has tried this," they are almost certainly looking at a tar pit. The corrective is one minute of Google: search `{idea} 2018-2024 startup` and read the first three post-mortems.

## The canonical tar pit list

### Meet-up-with-friends apps

The dream: a frictionless way to coordinate hanging out with people you actually like. The reality: friends already have group chats. The thing that "works" is iMessage, WhatsApp, or Discord — none of them designed for the use case, all good enough. New entrants cannot beat 2–3 incumbents that already have everyone's contacts plus a year of conversation history. Retention drops to zero after the first attempted plan that didn't happen.

### Local event discovery

The dream: Yelp for things to do tonight. The reality: events are bursty, the listings problem is unsolvable without paying a city's worth of curators, and the user shows up once a month at most. CAC ≈ LTV. Eventbrite ate the supply side; Yelp ate the discovery side; neither makes real money on this. Failed attempts: Plancast, Meetup-as-startup pivots, Sosh, Field Trip — see post-mortems.

### Unified communication inbox

The dream: one inbox for Slack + email + Twitter + WhatsApp + SMS. The reality: every platform actively prohibits this via terms of service and API gating, the user's true need is fewer notifications not more aggregation, and the value prop dies if any one source is missing. Multiple high-profile shutdowns. Read the IFTTT and Wavebox stories.

### Social planning

The dream: an app that picks the restaurant, the time, and the people. The reality: nobody trusts an algorithm to plan their social life; everyone already has a default friend who plans. Retention drops after the first week. Many tried; none survived.

### Generic "Uberize X" for fragmented services

The dream: marketplace for [haircuts / dog-walking / interior decoration / handyman / lawn care]. The reality: in non-network-effect categories, supply leaks (provider takes the customer off-platform), demand is rare per user (monthly at best), and the take rate that makes the marketplace work makes the providers leave. The categories that worked (Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash) had time-pressure, anonymous trust, and frequency. Most "Uberize X" categories have none of those.

### Other patterns worth watching

- **B2B "communication tool" replacements** for Slack/Teams from a feature-only angle.
- **AI-wrapper apps with no distribution advantage** — feature, not company.
- **Consumer social with no behavioral hook** — "Instagram but for X" without a new behavior.
- **Crypto-for-thing-that-doesn't-need-crypto** — most of these.

## Why tar pits look attractive

- Easy to explain ("It's Yelp for X").
- The pain point is real and named.
- Existing solutions are visibly bad.

But the *reason* existing solutions are visibly bad is usually that the market structure makes it impossible to be much better, not that nobody competent has tried. Always verify which.

## Schlep blindness — the inverted twin

Attribution: Paul Graham, *Schlep Blindness*.

The opposite mistake: founders dismiss ideas that look hard, boring, regulated, or unsexy — even though those are often the *best* ideas because the difficulty itself is the moat. Stripe was "yet another payments processor." PlanGrid was "drawing management software for construction sites." Brex required a year of regulatory work. These looked like tar pits to outsiders and were actually the opposite.

The diagnostic: if the founder is dismissing an idea because it's "boring" or "tedious" or "feels like real work," ask why. If the only answer is "it doesn't feel exciting," they are probably wrong. Boring + regulated + tedious is often where the venture-scale outcomes live, because competitors avoid the work.

## Tar pit vs. schlep — how to tell

| Signal | Tar pit | Schlep |
|---|---|---|
| Lots of failed predecessors | Yes (and all the same way) | Sometimes — but if so, all for the same boring reason that you can solve |
| Boring to work on | No, sounds fun | Yes |
| Acute pain for a named ICP | Often vague | Usually very specific |
| Founder has insider edge | Often no | Often yes — the schlep is why they're not blocked |
| Competitors are visibly bad | Yes — and the next ten will be too | Yes — but the next one with operational rigor wins |

When the diagnostic is ambiguous, the question that breaks the tie is: **what specifically would change about your approach versus the ten people who tried this before you?** If the answer is "nothing structural, we'll just execute better," it's a tar pit. If the answer is "we have data/distribution/regulatory access they didn't," it might be a schlep — keep digging.
