# Cofounder Frameworks

Attribution: Y Combinator (cofounder rules across multiple Startup School videos and the Cofounder Matching platform), Paul Graham (*How to Pick a Cofounder*), and the post-mortem corpus on cofounder disasters.

## YC's non-negotiable rules

1. **Work together on a meaningful project for at least 4 weeks before committing.** Founder relationships fail more often than marriages and on a faster timeline. You cannot evaluate a cofounder from coffee chats. You evaluate them by shipping with them under pressure. A 4-week trial with real stakes (an experiment, a real outreach push, an MVP build) reveals working style, conflict patterns, and follow-through.

2. **Near-equal equity for true cofounders.** 50/50, or 51/49 if you must have a tiebreaker. Lopsided splits (90/10, 70/30) between people who are both committing full-time and irreplaceable are a tell that someone is not actually a cofounder — they're an early hire being mislabeled. Resentment compounds. The data on cofounder disputes traces almost entirely to "I deserve more equity now that I see how much I'm doing."

3. **Vesting is non-negotiable.** Four years with a one-year cliff. This protects the company against a cofounder who walks at month six taking 50% of the cap table. No cofounder who refuses to vest is a real cofounder.

4. **Don't ship without a written founder agreement.** Equity, vesting, decision rights, what happens on departure. Templates exist (YC SAFE adjacent docs, Cooley GO). Do this before you have anything worth fighting over, because by then it's too late.

## Patterns to avoid

- **LinkedIn-stranger cofounders.** A person you matched with last month and have never shipped with is not a cofounder. They might become one — work together for 4 weeks first.
- **Family-member cofounders you can't fire.** Spouses, parents, siblings. The financial entanglement makes course corrections impossible. Many YC companies have done it; the majority of post-mortems on that path are brutal.
- **The 90/10 "I had the idea" split.** Ideas are worth ~5% of the value created in building a company. If both of you commit full-time, both of you are cofounders. If one of you isn't full-time, that person is an advisor with options, not a cofounder.
- **Hiring a contractor to build the v1.** This is not a cofounder strategy; it's a way to ship a v1 you cannot maintain or evolve. Either you (or an actual cofounder) build the v1, or you wait until you can.

## Sales hires before PMF — a refusal pattern

Founders MUST do early sales themselves. Founder-led sales is the only reliable way to learn what objections customers raise, what language closes them, and what they actually pay for. Hiring a salesperson pre-PMF is one of the most documented failure modes in the YC corpus: the company hires the wrong profile because it doesn't yet know what good looks like; the salesperson cannot get past objections the founder hasn't fielded; the founder loses the feedback loop. The system refuses to validate "hire a sales person" for any pre-PMF company. Bring the founder to the call.

## The 4-week trial protocol

Use this when a founder is considering committing to a cofounder. Do not commit before week 4.

- **Week 1.** Pick a concrete deliverable that requires both your skill sets. Could be a validation experiment, a landing page + outreach batch, an MVP build, or 20 customer interviews. Ship it together. Pay attention to: who initiates, who follows through, who edits, who escalates conflict productively.
- **Week 2.** Continue. Run customer conversations together — same call, both of you on. Debrief separately, then compare interpretations. Cofounders who interpret the same call radically differently and *don't reconcile* are a warning sign.
- **Week 3.** Run a real failure together. Pick something likely to break — a customer pitch you might lose, a technical decision under time pressure. Watch how they behave when it goes sideways. Easy weeks tell you nothing; the hard week tells you everything.
- **Week 4.** The hard conversation. Cover: equity (start with 50/50 and only deviate with explicit reason), vesting (4-year, 1-year cliff, non-negotiable), runway commitment (how long can each of you go without salary?), what happens if one of you wants out at month 8, life events that might pull either of you away. If you cannot have this conversation, you cannot cofound. Walk away.

## When you don't need a cofounder

Solo founding is harder but viable. Skip the cofounder search if:

- You are a domain expert who can learn enough engineering or design to ship v1, and you'd rather move alone than wait.
- You are technical with deep network in the target customer's domain — you can do customer development yourself.
- You've cofounded before and know your blind spots and how to hire around them.

The cost of solo founding is loneliness, slower decisions under uncertainty, and a thinner sounding board. The cost of the wrong cofounder is the company. The latter is much more expensive.
